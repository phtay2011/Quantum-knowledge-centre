More about the Euro

The Euro Currency is the single currency used by the European Monetary Union (EMU) and its member states, under the designated Single Monetary and Exchange Rate policies,

EMU has been established in a number of stages since 1990. The first stage removed the exchange controls of some of the member states. The second stage began at the beginning of 1994 and introduced the European Monetary Institute (EMI) which assumed a coordinating role between member states who, during this stage, were expected to make their central banks independent of their governments.

The third stage began in January 1998 and saw the participating states adopt the fixed translation rates (the rates defined for the conversion of an IN currency to the Head currency) that were needed to substitute the Euro for the Member States national currencies. During this stage, other requirements were defined to prepare for the introduction of the Euro. The European System of Central Banks (ESCB) was established, which comprised the European Central Bank (ECB) - to replace the EMI - and representatives of the national central banks.

The ECB was defined as the sole issuing authority for Euro bank notes, enabling participating states who had met the entry criteria of the third phase to start using the Euro currency.

The period from 1 January 1999 through 31 December 2001 was the convergence period when the first wave of participating Member States began using the Euro currency through the fixed translation rates. This period related to the policy of No Compulsion - No Prohibition (see below).

From the year 2002, the national currencies (or IN currencies) of the member states ceased to exist. At this stage, participating members were required to deactivate their national currency.

In Quantum Six, the Euro Currency is defined as the primary member (the head) of a Currency Group. The Euro Group consists of:

  • A name to identify the group (for example, EMU)
  • The IN currencies (the members of the EMU)
  • A Head currency (the currency that all IN currencies are translated into)

No Compulsion - No Prohibition policy

This term refers to the policy that exists during the convergence period. That is, all who wish to use the Euro are entitled to do so, however, no-one can be forced to use it except for the following situations where the Euro must be used:

  • Monetary and foreign exchange rate policy operations by the ESCB
  • Real Time Gross Settlement (RTGS) systems from each participating state interconnected by the wholesale payment system of the ESCB, known as TARGET
  • New issues of tradable public debt by the participating countries
  • EBA Clearing System and other clearing systems
