Overview (Commodity dealing example)

Your organization, Acme Cooling Inc (say), needs 202 metric tonnes (MT) of aluminum on 31 August for production of heat sinks.

On 31 July, London Metals Exchange (LME) post the future price for aluminum delivered on 31 August as USD2,075.00 per MT. The actual price for aluminum on 31 August may be different, so Acme want to eliminate the risk of such price fluctuation.

Your Corporate Finance department engages in a Fixed for Floating Commodity Swap contract with XYZ Bank, stating:

  • Acme will buy 202 MT at USD2,075/MT (the Fixed side), and
  • YYZ Bank will sell 202 MT at a floating price (the Floating side).

    The floating price is derived from the market price of Aluminum during the period of the contract by applying the predefined Average Commodity Price formula to the LME-supplied prices (from the LME End of Day Commodity Rate record).

The Commodity Swap deal:

  • is a financial deal that moves the risk of price fluctuation from Acme to XYZ Bank;
  • does not involve the buying or selling of aluminum (the physical deal*);
  • locks in a fixed price of USD2,075/MT for 202 MT on 31 July, eliminating price uncertainty between 1 August and 31 August; and
  • will have a Settlement Amount on 31 August of the difference between the actual price then and the locked-in fixed price multiplied by the agreed quantity.

On 31 August, suppose the price is set at USD2050/MT. The Settlement Amount of the Swap deal is therefore:

(2050 - 2075) x 202 = -5050.00

Acme has therefore lost USD5050 on this deal (if the price was over USD2075/MT, they would have gained).

However, when Acme execute the physical deal to buy the actual commodity for the going market price of USD2050/MT, their loss on the financial deal is offset, keeping the overall cost at USD2075.

*TIP: The Commodity Swap deal does not track the physical deal (the actual delivery of the commodity). That would typically be entered independently, as an Actual Cash flow deal.
