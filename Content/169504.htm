Historical VaR Methodologies

Previously, Historical Simulation results have been calculated by generating two simulated market values (for your chosen Position or reporting date) using our standard valuation models.

These two simulated market values use the market rates from two consecutive business days (during your chosen historical period) and your Historical Simulation result is simply the difference between the two simulated market values.

Relative Daily Return Calculation Method

The newer Relative Daily Return Historical Simulation result is based on the relative or percentage change in rates (or valuation inputs) between your two historic business dates:

NOTE: These percentage changes are calculated at the level of the relevant valuation input and not at the overall Market Value level.

Once this percentage change is determined, the resultant factor is then applied to the rate (or valuation input) for Position or reporting date

Comparing the Methods

The Crude Oil Worked example demonstrates one of the key theoretical benefits of the Relative Daily Return method. Where there are large changes in the levels of rates or prices across your time series, the new method automatically factors in the implicit historic level of volatility with respect to the current level of rates or prices.

In the example, the result of $206,792.95 can be seen to be more relevant or appropriate or "correct" with the oil price at $70.59 than the result of $59,000.

Another key theoretical benefit can be seen with respect to options.

If the organization in the example has a large call option with a strike of $70.50, then that option is very slightly "in the money". However, more importantly, we know that the valuation of that option is highly sensitive to changes in the underlying oil price. You would therefore expect that option position to generate a large VaR number (or Historical Simulation result).

Using the traditional method however, you would probably see a Simulation result of zero or very close to it. Valuing this call option once with the price of the underlying at $20.14 and then again with the price at $20.73 (both levels very deeply "out of the money") would simply generate two results of very close to zero, suggesting that the position carries very little risk when, in reality, it is considerable.

Conclusion

While still valid for most markets and conditions, the traditional method carries conceptual weaknesses where there are options in the current portfolio and/or where there have been significant changes in the levels of prices or rates across the specified observation period.
